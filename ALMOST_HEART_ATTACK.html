Almost Heart Attack

Jan 25, 2026 • 10 min read

Around the middle of my second standard, I guess I had chicken pox or something like that — and a disease called Kawasaki. Yep, like the bike brand.

It’s a disease where the blood vessels near your heart get inflamed, which *could* lead to a heart attack. Because of that, I was admitted to the hospital for two months — two weeks of that in the ICU. Not a huge deal though. I wasn’t on a ventilator or anything. They just had this fancy medicine that needed constant monitoring.

First Time in a Hospital

I was admitted to Kauvery Hospital, Trichy. It was kinda awesome. I don’t remember the exact floor — 3rd, 4th… nah, way higher.

When I first arrived with a referral letter from a doctor in my hometown, I wasn’t scared at all. Deepan back then didn’t understand the seriousness of the situation. I was just happy I didn’t have to go to school. (I was a topper, by the way.)

My dad asked me what I wanted from the canteen. I said “I don’t know” — classic me. That’s when I had tomato juice/soup for the first time. Man, that thing tasted heavenly.

Soon after, I was called in for the injection that connects the glucose bottle to your hand. They told my mom she couldn’t stay inside. She looked at me, and I just laughed and said, “It’s okay.” By then, I was already used to injections. Also… I think the nurse was pretty.

And just like that, the next two months of my life were booked. Future Deepan would one day write about this on a blog.

Hospital Life

Honestly, daily life in the hospital was kinda fun. I’d wake up to Robot and Man on Pogo. My mom would make bread soaked in milk and feed me in a bowl. Ahhh, good old days.

My room had two beds separated by a curtain. The TV was on my side (important detail). Most days, the other bed was empty.

I’d walk around the corridors, randomly press elevator buttons at the reception, and pretend to be asleep when relatives came to visit.

One morning, I overheard the doctor talking to my mom about my condition. That’s when I realized this was actually serious. Strangely, that didn’t bother me. What bothered me was him saying I shouldn’t eat oily food.

Guess what my dad did that night? He brought me chole fucking bhature. That man is a legend.

My Disciple

I kinda had a disciple in the hospital. A younger boy with a similar condition. Since I was in second standard and he wasn’t in school yet, I took it upon myself to mentor him.

I’d tell him not to sit in corridors and take him on walks. After a few weeks, he got discharged. Two years later, during my yearly checkup, I saw him again. He didn’t remember me — which hurt a little — but I was happy to see him.

Things That Stayed With Me

One day, walking through the corridor, I peeked into an open room. A lady was lying there, connected to a ventilator, tubes going into her lungs and mouth — barely clinging to life.

I don’t know what happened to her. I hope she’s fine today. Maybe one day I’ll find her and become friends.

Echo Tests & Underground Tunnels

Every week, I had to undergo an echo test. They’d put this smelly gel on my chest and move a probe around. On the screen, you could see this white inverted cone with black spots — like something straight out of a movie.

Three people would come with a wheelchair to take me. I’d argue saying I could walk. Eventually, I’d sit anyway — because who doesn’t like being pushed around like a king?

My room was in Kauvery Hospital, but the tests were done in Kauvery Heart Hospital. How do you get there? Underground tunnels. A -2 floor tunnel connecting both buildings. Cool as hell.

There was this doctor I nicknamed “Angry Nose”. He once scolded a nurse because she forgot to inform him I’d arrived. Apparently, he had to do my test personally. I felt special. Still annoyed though.

ICU Days

The medicine I needed wasn’t available locally. It was imported from abroad and cost a lot. Thankfully, my dad had good insurance.

One night, a nurse administered medicine at the wrong time. Worst night of my life. I was awake the whole night. My mom carried me on her shoulders, walking up and down the corridor.

Ironically, my aunt was the one who finally calmed me down. She still brags about “saving my life” to this day.

Once the medicine arrived, I was moved to the ICU for two weeks. Honestly? Luxury. Nurses cleaned me at 4 a.m., changed bedsheets, brought food. Everyone was kind.

My cousin gave me his PSP. I played nonstop. Absolute legend.

I used to mess with the ECG machine. Remove the probe from my toe and watch it go beep beep. Nurses were not amused.

Going Home

Parents weren’t allowed in the ICU at night. I don’t know where my mom slept. Probably the corridor. We had relatives nearby, but she never left. Thinking about it now — damn.

Eventually, I was moved back to my normal room. My disciple had already left. I was about to be discharged.

Two days before leaving, my favorite nurse quit. I said goodbye. She was awesome.

On discharge day, I said goodbye to everyone. Believe it or not, I was kinda popular among nurses.

Leaving the hospital after two months felt strange. I’d forgotten what the outside world felt like. Home felt weird after living a “luxury hospital life.”

Life went on. I passed second grade without writing final exams.
